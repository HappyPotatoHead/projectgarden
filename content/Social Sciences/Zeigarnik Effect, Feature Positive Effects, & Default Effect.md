# Zeigarnik Effect

>[!INFO] Zeigarnik Effect
>This theory postulates that people seldom forget incomplete or interrupted tasks. In other words, people remember incomplete tasks better than those that are completed. 

## How does it work
"Short-term memory refers to the memory systems in the brain involved in remembering pieces of information for a short period of time, often up to 30 seconds".[^1] Typically, when we want to retain information in our memory, we have to resort to rehearsing the information repeatedly. This process requires some mental effort; the more effort you exert, 
the better you would be at remembering the information. 

To deal with a mass amount of data, a myriad of tricks are invented with one of them being *Zeigarnik effect*. Many of us exploit this effect unknowingly; we constantly pull information back into our awareness to hold on to information in the short term. 

The effects of Zeigarnik effect, however, is not only short term. Goals that we still have yet to achieved will still loom in the corner of our memories for years. For instance, individuals who failed to complete their degrees may remember it for years.

Zeigarnik suggested that incomplete or interrupted tasks create underlying cognitive tension.[^2]. This means that we exert more mental effort and conduct more rehearsals to keep the tasks in our awareness. Regardless, once the unfinished task is finished, our mind can finally let itself occupied by another task. 

**Examples**
 - When you're about to go to bed, but you suddenly remember your laundry that has been piling on for days and demands immediate attention. 
 - You replied to a bunch of texts except one from your ex. You spend the rest of the weekend thinking about it.
 - Waiters remember customers' orders until they have been completed. 
## Research
In 1938, Zeigarnik published a paper, *On finished and unfinished tasks*, to further hone her theory. Through a series of experiments, Zeigarnik discovered more individuals who had been interrupted have a better remembrance of what they had been doing as opposed to those that completed their tasks. The participants were subjected to rather simple tasks such as placing beads on a string, solving puzzles, or solving mathematical problems. Half of the participants managed to finish the tasks uninterrupted. 

*See paper here:* https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2007-10344-025

In 1963, John Baddeley furthered explored Zeigarnik's idea in another experiment. The participants were told to solved anagrams in an allocated time. If the participants could not solve the anagram before the time was up, they would be given the answer. After the experiment, each participants were asked to recall the anagrams. It is found that participants were better at remembering words that they did not solve as opposed to the ones they had. 

*See paper here:* https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1080/17470216308416553

However, there have been researches that opposes this theory. Some researches have found that there are a variety of factors that affect the potency of this effect. For instance, there was a study by Oyama, Manalo and Nakatani, that showed that motivation plays a role in how well people remember information. 

## The good, the bad, and the ugly
### The good

Zeigarnik effect can be used to aid students in their studies. Although it may seem counterintuitive, momentary interruptions may be beneficial. Instead of repetitive recalling, students can review the notes a few times, and then take a break. Another way to exploit Zeigarnik effect is to break up the study session rather than attempting to complete everything in a single session. Students should practise incremental revision. 

Zeigarnik effect can also be used to overcome procrastination. Rather than trying to finish the task in a single session, break it up into multiple sessions, with each contributing a small segment to the final product. By doing this, the task will loom in our memory until the task is finished. *Focus on small victories.*
### The bad

Consumers may be tricked by advertisers and marketers with the Zeigarnik effect. Movie trailers are designed to tease people by showing only a teaser of information about its plot and characters. The trailer aims to draw attention but leaves consumers wanting more. Shows that end on a cliff-hanger create the same effects. To relieve the tension created, viewers have to remember to watch the next episode or the next season.
### The ugly

Zeignarnik effect may be harmful in the long run as, depending on the person, unfinished tasks may weigh heavily on the person's mind and creates stress which will lead to anxiety. Nonetheless, this may drive individuals to complete the tasks and relieve the stress created.

# Feature Positive Effects
>[!DEFINITION] Feature Positive Effects
> The presence of something (object, emotion, and state) is easier to detect as opposed to the absence of something.
## How does it work
*Series A*: $724, 947,421,843, 394, 411, 054, 646$
*Series B*: $349, 851, 274, 905, 772, 854, 113$

Looking that series A, what do these numbers have in common? 
- [?] Read the number out loud. 
The number 4 is present in each of the number. 

Now, look at series B, what do these numbers have in common?
The number 6 is absent in each of the number.

## The good, the bad and the ugly.
### The good
Prevention campaigns utilise this effect. The slogan *Smoking causes lung cancer* is more powerful than *No smoking leads to cancer-free life*. This is because the absence of cancer as *cancer-free* suggests does not warrant any course of action as opposed to *causes lung cancer*. 
### The bad
Those who employ checklists - auditors - are prone to FPE. Outstanding tax declarations are immediately obvious as they feature on their lists. However, what does not appear is the artistic fraud, such as what in Enron Scandal and the Ponzi scheme. The undertakings of 'rogue traders' - Nick Leeson and Jerome Kervial - are also absent. (Barings and Société Générale fell victim to). These kinds of financial vagaries are absent on any checklist.[^3]
### The ugly

Suppose a manufacturer manufactures a dubious condiment (*pink sauce*) with dubious nutritional values. On the label, the manufacturer may promote the benefits the condiment brings but omit important yet potentially fatal information. Most of the consumers would not notice its absence.

# Default effect
>[!DEFINITION] Default effect
> It is a concept that explains the tendency for an individual to accept the default option.

**How does it work**
The default option is the course of action chosen by individuals if he or she does not specify any other particular course of action. Firms, companies and advertisers exploit this phenomenon to drive customers to choose the most optimal outcome (*for the company*). There are researches conducted that showed making an option the default option increases the likelihood of it being chosen. The default can be categorised into two - mass defaults and personalised defaults.

### Mass default
>[!DEFINITION] Mass defaults
>This form of default effect is applied on all consumers of a product or service without taking into account each individual consumers preferences. 

Mass default is usually opted for when the company does not want to invest time and money into providing default options unique to each and every individual. Due to their nature, mass defaults is not satisfactory to some customers. Mass defaults can be divided further into 3 categories

| Mass defaults   | Description                                                                                                                            |
| --------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Benign defaults | This is the company's best guess on the customers' best guess                                                                          |
| Random defaults | The default option is arbitrarily assigned to customers. This is only opted for when any other strategy will cause harm to the company |
| Hidden options  | The company presents a default option as the only choice while there are other 'hidden' options.                                       |
### Personalised defaults
>[!DEFINITION] Personalised defaults
>The firm tailors the default option to meet respective consumer's preferences. 

This form of default option requires the firm to acquire data on their customers. Personalised defaults can be divided into 3.


| Personalised defaults | Description                                                                                                                                                             |
| --------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Smart defaults        | Customer information such as demographic is utilised to generate the default option                                                                                     |
| Persistent defaults   | The default option is generated with the data obtained from consumers' past purchases and preferences. *This is the data breach most individuals are concerned about*   |
| Adaptive defaults     | The company updates each customer's profile in real-time, allowing them to target customers when there is a lack of data on consumers' past preferences and demographic |
## So why does it happen
There are a number of explanations that have been offered.
### Cognitive effort 
When presented with multiple choices, an individual has to exerted quite the mental effort to evaluate the outcomes of each choice. With the presence of a default option, an individual is given the change to relieve the tension. 
### Switching cost
An individual may feel as though the cost of diverging from the default may surmount the benefits from choosing the default option. The cost of diverging may be but not limited to:
1. Time 
2. Consultancy fees
3. Fees
