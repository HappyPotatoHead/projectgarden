# Anchor Bias

>[!DEFINITION] Anchor Bias
>We make a piece of information obtained earlier in time as the point of reference for other decisions down the line. This will skew our judgement and prevents us from making actual beneficial decisions properly.

## How does it work?

After weeks of hard work, you decide to reward yourself with a gift. "What's the harm in splurging once in a while", you thought. You came across a pair of handbags - 3,000\$ - but they cost much more than your budget. You decided to keep shopping and you came across a cheaper handbag which costs 2,500$. "What a bargain", you thought to yourself and you irrationally purchased the handbag without further thought. Unbeknownst to you, there are better deals on the market. But hey, it's still cheaper right?

So why did you jump the wagon? Why the rush? Because the price-tag of 3,000$ has etched itself in your mind - your anchor point. Despite knowing there might have been better deals in the market, you still based your decision on the anchor point. 

Anchor bias can influence many aspects of one's life such as salary. Imagine another scenario (it's getting tiring, isn't it) where you're negotiating a raise with your boss. You gave yourself a pep talk in the bathroom and walked into his office with confidence. However, confidence quickly drained from your body and you hesitated to make an initial offer. That is your first mistake. Based on anchoring bias researches, you may get much better out-come simply by naming the digits first. Your boss will pick that up as the anchor and starts negotiating your salary based on that anchor. Any future offers will be based on that.[^1]

Anchors can originate from **external** and *internal* sources
1. **External** 
	- Provided by others
	- *See example above*
2. **Internal**
	- Provided by beliefs, experiences, or contextual clues. 
	- If the people you grew up with exercised a lot, you may set a standard level of exercise in adulthood.

>[!WARNING] The less we know
>The less we know about a topic, the more likely we are to fall for anchoring bias. When there is a lack of information, we are more likely to be influenced by anchors.

*Other Examples* [[The Anchor Bias, Hasty Generalisation, & Loss Aversion#^ad3c33|(n)]]
1. _How much television should your children watch each day?_ If you watched lots of TV as a kid, a few hours might seem acceptable.
2. _How long do you expect to live?_ If your parents were both very long-lived, you might automatically expect to live a long life. Because of this anchoring point, you might ignore the fact that your parents lived a healthier, more active lifestyle than yours.

## Why does it happen

### Justification 1 -  Anchor and Adjust

In 1974, Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky published a paper - Judgement under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases. They theorised that people begin with some initial value, or starting value, and then adjust the value from there when trying to make estimates or predictions. The adjustments aren't usually significant, causing less-than-desirable decision-making.

## Research

According to research done by Gustavo Saposnik, Donald Redelmeier, Christian C.Ruff & Philippe N. Tobler, at least one cognitive factor or bias was present in studies conducted. According to studies that evaluate at least 2 cognitive biases, it is found that half of the physicians in the studies were affected by at least one cognitive. Personality traits such as tolerance to risk or ambiguity is commonly studied, whereas the framing effects and overconfidence takes the spot for common cognitive biases. However, a wide variability exists in the reported prevalence of cognitive biases. For instance, after conducting analysis on the studies that included several cognitive biases, the availability bias ranged from 7.8 to 75.6%, and the anchor bias from 5.8 to 87.8%

*See the paper here:* [Cognitive biases associated with medical diagnosis](https://bmcmedinformdecismak.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12911-016-0377-1#Sec7)

Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky conducted a demonstration of the anchoring effect. In one experiment, each participant were told to spin a wheel to determine the number between 0 and 100. The subjects were then instructed to estimate various quantities - the percentage of African countries in the United Nation. The participants were first told to indicate where the number determined was higher or lower than their estimates and then told to estimate the quantity. It is found that the arbitrary numbers determined had an effect on the estimates. For instance, the median estimates of the percentage of African Countries in the United Nation were 25% and 45% for those who received 10 and 65.  

Kahneman and Tversky conducted another experiment to prove that anchoring does not occur only when the subject has been given a starting point. Two groups of high school students were told to estimate the product of the numerical expression that was written. In one group, this equation were shown: 
$$8\times7\times6\times5\times4\times3\times2\times1$$
In another group, this equation were shown:
$$1\times2\times3\times4\times5\times6\times7\times8$$
Kahneman and Tversky argued that the students performed initial calculation and then est
imated the actual product by extrapolation or adjustment. It is also found that the number that are in descending order yield larger estimations than numbers that are in ascending order. (The median estimate for the first group was 2,250 while the median estimate for the second group was 512. Of course, both were wrong.)

*See the paper here:* [Judgement under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.185.4157.1124)
